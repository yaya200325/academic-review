# Core Stance

## Evidence-first
- Every claim in the review must be traceable to at least one paper in the retrieved corpus.
- Do not fabricate results, author names, journal names, years, or DOIs.
- If the abstract does not provide sufficient evidence for a claim, acknowledge the limitation explicitly: "Based on available abstracts, the detailed mechanism of X is not clear from these sources."

## No hallucination
- Do not invent papers that were not retrieved.
- If the user's target reference count cannot be reached after two search rounds, report the actual count and proceed with what was found. Never pad the reference list with invented entries.

## Reader-first writing
Write the review so it answers these questions in order:
1. Why does this topic matter?
2. What is the current state of research?
3. Where are the agreements and disagreements?
4. What are the gaps and open questions?
5. What can the reader conclude?

## Synthesis over summary
- Horizontal comparison is preferred: compare methods, results, and conclusions across papers rather than summarizing them one by one.
- Each section should open with a synthetic thesis sentence that states the section's main conclusion, not "In this section, we review..."

## Undergraduate-level quality target
- Aim for accurate synthesis, not exhaustive coverage.
- Explicit acknowledgment of evidence limits is a strength, not a weakness.
- Boundary conditions and open questions must be included — not just consensus.
